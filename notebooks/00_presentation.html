1. Project Introduction & Background¶

Introduction: In Search of Lost River Memories¶

Rivers are the cradle of civilization. However, their flow is not eternal. Throughout the eons, rivers meander and drastically change their form. Water may have long since vanished from places where civilizations once flourished.

But what if those memories have been passed down to the present day as "place names"?

Our adventure began with such a romantic hypothesis. Land names and folklore might be time capsules of the soul that remain rooted in places even after people have left and rivers have dried up.

The name of this project, "Tamagawa to Z," derives from Japan's "Tamagawa River" where our team members live. This river, which once repeatedly flooded, left the same place name "Todoroki" on both its banks. The place names still connect the two lands that the river divided and time separated. Fascinated by the storytelling power of these place names, we decided to embark on a journey to search for the same miracle in the distant Amazon lands.

Our Compass: LLM and Multilingual Toponymy¶

Amazon's history is a crossroads of diverse languages. The languages of indigenous peoples and immigrants have mixed together, changed over time, and hidden the true nature of the land behind a complex veil.

Our greatest weapon is the power of LLM that breaks through this language barrier and the knowledge of toponymy (the study of place names).

We don't simply search for words meaning "water." We had LLMs interpret countless place names scattered throughout the Amazon in multilingual contexts, comprehensively judging their etymology, semantic similarity, and historical background, practicing what could be called "linguistic taxonomy." This is the work of excavating the "land's memory" sleeping deep within words.

Through this process, we created the world's one and only "Multilingual Toponym Dictionary." This is our unique compass in our long and arduous exploration journey.

Route to Discovery¶

We chose Acre State, Brazil, filled with possibilities for undiscovered findings, as the stage for our adventure. This land, where "new ground surfaces" are continuously appearing due to deforestation and development, is truly a frontier where numerous geoglyphs have been recently found from satellite images.

Our route is as follows:

1. Listening to Voices Sleeping in Maps: With our created "Multilingual Toponym Dictionary" in hand, we picked up place names from Acre State in OSM (OpenStreetMap) and listed places that might be related to water.

2. Setting Course to the Past: Our goal is to explore "lost rivers." Therefore, we narrowed down to lands that are a certain distance away from current rivers and have "low wetland levels," suggesting they were possible residential areas in the past. To improve the accuracy of this narrowing down, we adjusted parameters while discussing with LLM (O3 Pro) itself, using how well we could reproduce known ruins as an indicator. It was like experiencing a discussion about unknown sea charts with an AI, an experienced and seasoned navigator.

3. Relying on Stars: Eight selected candidate sites. We scored them from multiple perspectives: feature matching combining topography and toponymy at known ruins, possibility of destruction/burial by artifacts, unexplored survey areas, topographical advantages from satellite images, mythological folklore, and more.

The Promised Land: Ramal Olho D'água¶

After numerous analyses and dialogues with AI, we finally arrived at one place.

Site Name Ramal Olho D'água
Coordinates -9.839247, -68.498725

This place is located in a true "blank zone," away from known ruin clusters. Satellite data suggests an ideal environment where "micro-highlands" and "low wetlands" spread in a mosaic pattern, suitable for ancient people to build settlements.

And when we investigated folklore related to this land using the place name "Olho D'Água (Eye of Water)" as a clue, we found in the materials of indigenous protection organizations in Acre State that indigenous people once lived around this very "Olho D'Água."

As a result of following the memory of place names excavated by AI, various perspectives strongly suggest that ruins sleep in this place.
